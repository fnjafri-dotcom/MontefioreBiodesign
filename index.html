About the Program

The Montefiore Einstein Innovation Biodesign Training Program is a unique one-year experience that seeks to train the next generation of clinician innovators. Given the high burden of chronic diseases in the Bronx, and the corresponding challenges faced by patients and providers, the program's objectives are to:

  1. Develop and implement innovative health technologies that improve outcomes (clinical, process, economic) for patients, providers, and the Montefiore Einstein health system.
  2. Contribute to presentations, publications, grant funding, and institutional intellectual property.
  3. Contribute to an innovation-based ecosystem at Montefiore Einstein comprised of didactics, mentoring, and sustainable product development and implementation.

Month 1 — September 2026: Orientation, Teams & Needs Finding

Theme: "A good problem is worth a hundred solutions." This month is about setup and disciplined needs identification — not solutions yet. Competencies addressed: #1 (digital health innovations).

Learning objectives — by the end of this month you can:

1. Explain the biodesign innovation cycle (identify → invent → implement) and where AI accelerates each phase.
2. Distinguish a well-characterized clinical need statement (population + problem + outcome) from a solution in disguise.
3. Apply Thiel's "zero to one" and Christensen's disruption frameworks to critique one existing digital health product.
4. Begin a literature and stakeholder discovery workflow using PubMed, ClinicalTrials.gov, Crunchbase, and FDA digital health reports.

Read Zero to One — Peter Thiel · Ch. 1–4 (finish book by end of Month 2)

Why this reading: frames innovation as creating something new (0→1) rather than copying what exists (1→n) — the mindset you need before picking a project.

Key question — click to reflect, then post your answer in the discussion below

Thiel asks: "What important truth do very few people agree with you on?" Answer it for healthcare in the Bronx — what do you believe about care delivery here that most of your colleagues would push back on?

Read The Innovator's Dilemma — Clay Christensen · Intro + Ch. 1

Why this reading: explains why large, well-run institutions (like health systems) systematically miss disruptive technologies — and how innovators exploit that.

Key question

Christensen shows disk-drive leaders failing because they listened too well to their best customers. What is the healthcare equivalent of "listening too well to your best customers" at an academic medical center?

Read NEJM Catalyst — The Clinician Role in Health Care Delivery and Innovation

Why this reading: argues clinicians are uniquely positioned to spot the needs that matter — the premise of this entire program.

Key question

The article describes barriers that keep clinicians from innovating. Which barrier is most real in your own department, and what would remove it?

🧠 Quick Knowledge Check (3 questions, instant feedback)

1. Which of these is a well-formed biodesign need statement?

A need statement names a population, a problem, and an outcome — and never embeds a solution. "An app that…" is a solution in disguise.

2. In Christensen's framework, a disruptive innovation typically starts by…

Disruptors gain a foothold in low-end or new-market segments that incumbents ignore, then improve until they displace them.

3. Thiel's "zero to one" question for any new venture is essentially:

Going from 0→1 means creating something new rather than iterating on what exists (1→n) — grounded in a contrarian insight.
